## 1. Monetary Policy and RBI

The Monetary Policy Committee (MPC) of the Reserve Bank of India met in February 2026 and decided to keep the repo rate unchanged at 5.25%. This followed a cumulative reduction of 125 basis points in the policy repo rate since February 2025, as inflationary pressures moderated. The RBI complemented the rate cuts with an injection of durable liquidity: cash reserve ratio cuts releasing approximately Rs 2.5 lakh crore, open market operations (OMO) amounting to Rs 6.95 lakh crore, and a forex swap of around $25 billion.

The MPC is a 6-member committee that decides the policy repo rate. It was constituted under the RBI Act, 1934 (amended in 2016). India's inflation targeting framework sets the target at 4% with a tolerance band of plus or minus 2 percentage points (range: 2% to 6%). This framework was recommended by the Urjit Patel Committee. The current mandate was effective until March 31, 2026; the Government renews the target every five years in consultation with the RBI.

Key RBI policy rate instruments: the repo rate is the rate at which the RBI lends short-term funds to banks against collateral. The Reverse Repo Rate is the rate at which the RBI absorbs liquidity from banks; the rate is fixed by RBI. The Standing Deposit Facility (SDF) replaced the Fixed Rate Reverse Repo as the liquidity corridor's floor. SDF is an overnight liquidity absorption mechanism with no collateral requirement, giving the RBI flexibility to increase its duration. The Marginal Standing Facility (MSF) is the ceiling of the corridor, at which banks can borrow overnight funds from the RBI above the repo rate.

The Cash Reserve Ratio (CRR) is the portion of deposits that banks must keep with the RBI in cash. The Statutory Liquidity Ratio (SLR) is the portion of deposits kept in liquid assets (gold, cash, government securities). Open Market Operations (OMO) involve the buying and selling of government securities by the RBI to control liquidity.

## 2. Banking and Financial System

Scheduled Commercial Banks (SCBs) must maintain a Capital to Risk-Weighted Assets Ratio (CRAR) of 9%, as per RBI norms. The chart tracking trends in GNPAs of SCBs and CRAR shows that Gross Non-Performing Assets have been on a declining trajectory. The GNPA ratio for SCBs reached 2.6% — a multi-year low — reflecting improved asset quality following the resolution cycle and IBC recoveries.

The Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code (IBC) established a time-bound resolution framework. Priority sector lending requirements mandate that 40% of a bank's Adjusted Net Bank Credit (ANBC) must flow to priority sectors, which include agriculture, micro, small and medium enterprises (MSMEs), export credit, education, housing, social infrastructure, renewable energy, and others.

The Non-Bank Financial Companies (NBFCs) invest in non-SLR bonds as part of their portfolio alongside bank lending. Co-lending norms have been introduced to facilitate collaboration between banks and NBFCs for priority sector credit. The credit-deposit (CD) ratio indicates the share of deposits being used for lending — a higher CD ratio reflects higher credit growth but a lower liquidity buffer; a lower CD ratio indicates conservative lending and higher liquidity.

UPI (Unified Payments Interface) transaction data appears as a leading indicator of urban domestic demand in the PLFS and economic nowcasting models. CBDC (Central Bank Digital Currency) pilots have been underway under RBI's mandate to test digital currency for retail and wholesale segments. The Financial Inclusion Index rose to 67 in 2025, a 24.3% increase, reflecting adequate credit access for vulnerable groups.

India's financial system has been characterised by healthy credit growth, comfortable liquidity conditions, and strong corporate balance sheets. The flow of financial resources to the commercial sector from non-bank sources has been tracked as a measure of overall financial system activity.

## 3. Indian Economy Current Data

The government estimated a nominal GDP growth rate of 10% for 2026-27 (real growth plus inflation). The Economic Survey 2025-26 references the Nowcasting Model for quarterly GDP growth tracking. Sectoral growth in H1 FY26 was higher than pre-pandemic trends in most sectors. Growth in Manufacturing GVA deflator remained moderate. The share of manufacturing remains stable in real terms. The Economic Survey references a potential growth reassessment for India's medium-term trajectory.

Trends in CPI Inflation Components are tracked in Chart I.XX of the Economic Survey 2025-26. Monthly CPI (headline, core and food) inflation trajectory shows moderation. Components tracked include vegetables, pulses, and spices which exhibit high volatility; the Economic Survey references policy efforts to reduce volatility in pulses prices. Average Annual Headline Inflation data and Global Headline Inflation comparisons are included in the Survey.

WPI (Wholesale Price Index) has inbuilt bias due to double or multiple counting of the same product across production stages. WPI does not include exports and imports as separate categories. WPI also excludes the service sector, which accounts for approximately 55% of GDP. A committee recommended transitioning from WPI to PPI (Producer Price Index). The PPI measures average change in the price a producer receives for goods and services sold in domestic markets or exports. Output PPI covers goods at the producer stage.

The IIP (Index of Industrial Production) is the key measure of manufacturing and industrial performance on a monthly basis.

## 4. Fiscal Framework

The Union Budget for FY26 (February 2025) achieved a fiscal deficit of 4.8% of GDP, against the budgeted target of 4.9%. The Budget 2026-27 targets a fiscal deficit of 4.3% of GDP, lower than the revised estimate of 4.4% of GDP for FY25-26. This represents the fulfilment of a 2021 commitment to reduce the Union fiscal deficit by more than half (Chart I.22).

The fiscal deficit path: FY25 actual 4.8% → FY26 RE 4.4% → FY27 BE 4.3% of GDP.

Revenue deficit in 2026-27 is targeted at 1.5% of GDP, similar to the revised estimate of 1.5% for 2025-26. Primary deficit trajectory shows improvement; chart data from Economic Survey shows values declining across 2022-23, 2023-24, 2024-25, 2025-26 RE, and 2026-27 BE. The government aims to reduce outstanding central liabilities to around 50% of GDP by March 2031. In 2026-27, outstanding liabilities are estimated at 55.6% of GDP.

The Fiscal Responsibility and Budget Management (FRBM) Act, 2003 requires the central government to progressively reduce its outstanding debt, revenue deficit, and fiscal deficit, and to give three-year rolling targets.

Total government expenditure in 2026-27 is estimated at Rs 53,47,315 crore, which is 7.7% higher than the revised estimate of 2025-26. Interest payments account for 26% of total expenditure and 40% of revenue receipts. Non-tax receipts (other than borrowings) in 2026-27 are estimated at Rs 36,51,547 crore, about 7.2% higher than the revised estimate of 2025-26. Tax revenue is expected to increase by 8% over the revised estimate for 2025-26.

The government's receipts structure (from the Budget at a Glance): Borrowings and Liabilities: 24%, Corporation Tax: 18%, Income Tax: approximately 18-19%, and other components including Non-Tax Revenue, Customs, GST, Union Excise Duties.

Disinvestment target for 2026-27: Rs 80,000 crore. This marks the first increase in the disinvestment target after five consecutive years of downward revisions and shortfalls. The revised estimate for disinvestment in 2025-26 was Rs 33,837 crore against the budgeted Rs 47,000 crore. In 2025-26, the government is estimated to meet 71.9% of its disinvestment target (Rs 47,000 crore). Recoveries of Loans for 2026-27 BE: Rs 38,397 crore. Total receipts excluding borrowings for 2026-27 BE: Rs 36,51,547 crore.

Capital expenditure push: the government has emphasised a deliberate shift toward capital formation and human development as the basis of fiscal credibility, in contrast to cash transfers which crowd out capital expenditure. States have increased revenue deficits; the Centre's Special Assistance to States for Capital Expenditure/Investment (SASCI) scheme incentivises states to maintain capital spending.

Sovereign 10-year bond yields in India have declined relative to US bond yields (Chart I in Economic Survey), with a lower risk premium indicating improved macroeconomic credibility.

Income Tax: For assessment year 2026-27, there is no change in income tax slabs from the previous year. Minimum Alternate Tax (MAT) rate is being reduced from 15% to 14% in Budget 2026-27.

## 5. External Sector

India's current account deficit remains moderate at 0.8% of GDP in H1 FY26. India's external sector is placed comfortably in the short run, according to the Economic Survey 2025-26.

Forex reserves: cover over 11 months of imports as of January 16, 2026. The reserves cover approximately 94.0% of the external debt outstanding as of the end of September 2025, providing a comfortable buffer. Gold reserves have reached $102.3 billion (as per RBI's latest foreign exchange reserves data referenced in current affairs PDFs).

The Net International Investment Position (NIIP) and external debt position of India are tracked in Charts V.7 through V.12 of the Economic Survey. India's external debt position is compared with major debtor countries globally.

Trade deficit: India runs a trade deficit in goods. Its net trade surplus in services and remittances is not sufficient to offset the goods deficit. India depends on foreign capital flows to maintain a healthy balance of payments. Remittances serve as a key source of external funding for the current account. The rupee underperformed in 2025. The Economic Survey notes that the rupee's valuation does not accurately reflect India's stellar economic fundamentals — the rupee is described as punching below its weight. Currency stability during geopolitical crises requires healthy capital flows.

US tariff impact: the United States imposed an additional penal tariff of 25% on most of India's merchandise exports on top of the reciprocal tariff of 25% announced in April (referenced in trade section of CA PDFs). India was expected to be among the early winners in the new tariff regime despite this.

India's long-run external challenge is identified as generating sustained external surpluses through exports, productivity, and financial depth to finance investment cheaply and stably at home.

India's Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) policy and returns to FDI are discussed in the context of Global Value Chains (GVC) and currency strength determinants.

## 6. Financial Inclusion

PM Jan Dhan Yojana (PMJDY): nearly 56 crore beneficiaries as of the data referenced in the CA PDFs. The overdraft amount sanctioned under PMJDY is Rs 10,000 per account, which is classified as MUDRA loans under PMMY (Pradhan Mantri Mudra Yojana).

MUDRA (Pradhan Mantri Mudra Yojana — PMMY): key features include collateral-free loans with no collateral security or guarantor required. The PMJDY overdraft of Rs 10,000 is bundled under MUDRA classification.

Financial Inclusion Index rose to 67 in 2025, a 24.3% increase from the previous reading. Estimated poverty: 29.5% of India's population (from CA PDF data). Urban poverty line: Rs 816 per month (Monthly Per Capita Expenditure reference). Rural poverty line is lower. India's approach to poverty measurement uses the Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI) methodology alongside income-based measures.

UPI transaction growth is tracked as a key indicator of urban and rural demand. Two-wheeler vehicle sales and FMCG rural volume sales serve as rural demand indicators. Domestic air passenger traffic, passenger vehicle sales, and urban FMCG volume sales are tracked as urban demand indicators.

PLFS (Periodic Labour Force Survey) has a revised sampling design to generate monthly and quarterly unemployment estimates. Broader coverage with larger sample sizes and improved designs has been introduced to enable district-level estimates for both PLFS and ASUSE by 2026.

Credit-deposit ratios: a higher CD ratio indicates a higher share of deposits being used for lending (reflecting higher credit growth but lower liquidity buffer). A lower CD ratio indicates conservative lending and higher liquidity, but may signal inefficient use of deposits. Sectoral deployment of credit by SCBs is tracked in RBI's credit deployment data. Non-food credit growth (NFC) trend shows a recovery from the FY20-21 lows. Outstanding credit of SCBs shows upward momentum consistent with overall growth dynamics.

Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana was announced on August 28, 2014 and extended. It offers zero-balance accounts, RuPay debit cards, and accidental insurance coverage, and serves as the cornerstone of the Direct Benefit Transfer (DBT) mechanism. The scheme enabled financial inclusion by bringing the unbanked into the formal financial system and is central to the government's financial inclusion architecture. PMJDY accounts serve as conduits for welfare transfers including PM-KISAN and PMSBY (Pradhan Mantri Suraksha Bima Yojana).
