# RBI DEPR 2026 — Source Document 1: Monetary Policy & Banking

## 1. RBI's Monetary Policy Framework

The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) operates monetary policy through the Monetary Policy Committee (MPC), constituted under the RBI Act, 1934 (amended in 2016). The MPC has 6 members: 3 from the RBI (Governor, Deputy Governor, and one officer) and 3 external members appointed by the Government of India.

**Inflation Target:** The MPC targets CPI inflation at 4% with a tolerance band of ±2% (i.e., 2% to 6%). This target was recommended by the Urjit Patel Committee. The inflation mandate is reviewed every 5 years in consultation with the RBI.

**Current Policy Rate (February 2026 MPC Meeting):** The MPC decided to keep the repo rate unchanged at **5.25%**. This follows a cumulative reduction of 125 basis points since February 2025 as inflationary pressures moderated.

## 2. RBI's Key Monetary Instruments

### Policy Corridor (Liquidity Adjustment Facility — LAF)
- **Repo Rate (5.25%):** Rate at which RBI lends short-term funds to banks against collateral. The floor for borrowing costs.
- **Standing Deposit Facility (SDF):** The floor of the LAF corridor. SDF replaced the Fixed Rate Reverse Repo as the LAF corridor's floor. It is an overnight liquidity absorption mechanism (RBI absorbs liquidity from banks without giving collateral). The rate is not fixed by the market — it is fixed by RBI.
- **Marginal Standing Facility (MSF):** Rate at which banks can borrow overnight from RBI against approved securities, above the repo rate. Acts as the ceiling of the LAF corridor.

### Reserve Requirements
- **Cash Reserve Ratio (CRR):** Portion of Net Demand and Time Liabilities (NDTL) that banks must keep with the RBI in cash. CRR does not earn interest. In FY25–26, the RBI injected durable liquidity via CRR cuts worth ₹2.5 lakh crore.
- **Statutory Liquidity Ratio (SLR):** Portion of NDTL that banks must maintain in liquid assets — gold, cash, or approved government securities. SLR investments earn returns and are eligible for repo.

### Open Market Operations (OMO)
RBI buys or sells government securities in the open market to inject or absorb durable liquidity. In FY25–26, OMOs injected ₹6.95 lakh crore into the system. OMOs are a durable (permanent) liquidity tool, unlike repo which is temporary.

### Forex Swap
RBI conducted forex swaps of approximately $25 billion in FY25–26 to inject rupee liquidity while managing the exchange rate.

## 3. Monetary Policy Transmission

The RBI's policy rate cuts since February 2025 (cumulative 125 bps) have been effectively transmitted to market rates:
- Declining sovereign 10-year yields serve as benchmarks for borrowing costs across the economy.
- Lower yields act as fiscal stimulus by reducing government borrowing costs.
- Credit growth is described as "respectable" and corporate balance sheets are strong.

The transmission mechanism works through: repo rate → interbank call money rate → lending rates (MCLR/external benchmark) → credit demand → investment → output.

## 4. Banking Sector — Prudential Norms

### Capital Adequacy
- **CRAR (Capital to Risk-Weighted Assets Ratio):** Scheduled Commercial Banks (SCBs) must maintain a CRAR of **9%** as per RBI norms (above the Basel III minimum of 8%). This is the primary buffer against insolvency.
- **PCR (Provisioning Coverage Ratio):** The ratio of provisions held against NPAs to gross NPAs. A higher PCR indicates better loss absorption capacity.

### Asset Quality
- **Gross NPA (GNPA) ratio of SCBs:** As of FY26, the GNPA ratio stands at approximately **2.6%** — a multi-year low, reflecting resolution through the IBC (Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code) mechanism.
- **Net NPA (NNPA):** After netting out provisions. NNPA is well below GNPA, indicating adequate provisioning.
- Trends in GNPAs of SCBs and CRAR are improving — performance indicators of SCBs have strengthened.

### Priority Sector Lending (PSL)
Banks must direct **40% of Adjusted Net Bank Credit (ANBC)** to priority sectors. Priority sectors include:
- Agriculture
- Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises (MSMEs)
- Export Credit
- Education
- Housing
- Social Infrastructure
- Renewable Energy
- Others (weaker sections)

Non-SLR bonds and bank investments outside these categories do not count for PSL.

### Credit-Deposit (CD) Ratio
A higher CD ratio indicates that a larger share of deposits is being used for lending, reflecting higher credit growth but lower liquidity buffer. A lower CD ratio indicates conservative lending and higher liquidity. Co-lending norms allow banks and NBFCs to jointly originate loans.

## 5. Payment Systems

India's payment infrastructure is among the most advanced globally:

- **UPI (Unified Payments Interface):** Real-time mobile payment system operated by NPCI (National Payments Corporation of India). UPI transaction volume is a key indicator of domestic demand in the Economic Survey's nowcasting model.
- **RTGS (Real Time Gross Settlement):** For high-value transactions (₹2 lakh and above). Settlement is real-time and final.
- **NEFT (National Electronic Funds Transfer):** For lower-value transactions. Operates in batches throughout the day. Available 24x7 since December 2019.
- **IMPS (Immediate Payment Service):** 24x7 interbank fund transfer system for retail payments.

## 6. Financial Stability & Regulatory Architecture

- **RBI as systemic regulator:** Oversees monetary policy, banking supervision, payment systems, and external sector management.
- **Financial Stability Report (FSR):** RBI's semi-annual assessment of systemic risk, banking health, and credit outlook.
- **Integrated Ombudsman Scheme (2021):** Merged the banking, NBFC, and digital payments ombudsman schemes into one for easier grievance redressal.
- **Forex Reserves:** As of January 2026, India's forex reserves cover over **11 months of imports** and approximately **94% of external debt** outstanding (as of September 2025) — indicating comfortable external sector buffers.
- **Gold Reserves:** India's gold reserves reached **$102.3 billion** (as per latest RBI data).

---

## 7. Key RBI Initiatives and Regulatory Changes

- **SDF replaced Fixed Rate Reverse Repo:** This structural change in 2022 gave RBI more flexibility in liquidity management — banks park surplus liquidity with RBI without receiving collateral.
- **External Benchmark Lending Rate (EBLR):** Since October 2019, floating rate loans for retail and MSME are linked to external benchmarks (repo rate or T-bill rate), improving monetary transmission speed.
- **Co-lending Model:** Enables NBFCs and banks to co-originate priority sector loans, improving credit flow to underserved segments.
- **High Level Committee on Banking for Viksit Bharat (2026–27 Budget):** Set up to comprehensively review the banking sector in the context of India's Viksit Bharat 2047 goals.
