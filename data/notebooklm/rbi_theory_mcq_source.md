## 1. Macroeconomics: IS-LM, AD-AS, Phillips Curve, Monetary Transmission

### IS-LM Mechanics
The IS curve traces combinations of output (Y) and interest rate (r) where the goods market clears: Y = C + I + G. Investment is interest-sensitive (I = I₀ − br), so a higher r reduces I, reducing Y — the IS curve slopes downward. The LM curve traces combinations of Y and r where the money market clears: M/P = L(Y, i). Money demand rises with income (transaction motive) and falls with interest rate (opportunity cost). LM slopes upward. Equilibrium is simultaneously determined at the IS-LM intersection.

Fiscal policy shifts IS rightward (increase in G or cut in T). The crowding-out effect: higher G raises income, raises money demand, raises interest rate, which reduces private investment. Full crowding out occurs in a vertical LM (classical case); zero crowding out occurs in a horizontal LM (liquidity trap). Monetary policy shifts LM rightward. If LM is nearly horizontal (liquidity trap), monetary policy is ineffective — the classic Keynesian case.

### AD-AS Framework
The AD curve is derived from the IS-LM model: as the price level falls, real money supply rises, LM shifts right, equilibrium Y rises — hence a downward-sloping AD. The short-run AS curve slopes upward (sticky wages, misperceptions theory, menu costs). The long-run AS curve is vertical at potential output Y* (flexible prices, full employment). A supply shock (oil price rise) shifts SRAS leftward, producing stagflation — higher inflation and lower output simultaneously, a combination the standard Phillips curve cannot explain.

### Phillips Curve: Short Run and Long Run
The original Phillips curve (A.W. Phillips, 1958) showed an empirical inverse relationship between unemployment and wage inflation in the UK. Samuelson and Solow translated this to an inflation-unemployment trade-off for policy use. The expectations-augmented Phillips curve (Friedman-Phelps): π = πᵉ − α(u − u*), where u* is the natural rate of unemployment (NAIRU). If the central bank tries to hold unemployment below u*, it must continuously raise inflation — the long-run Phillips curve is vertical at u*. Adaptive expectations: agents update inflation expectations based on past inflation. Rational expectations (Lucas): anticipated monetary policy has no real effects; only unexpected policy matters.

MCQ trap: The exam frequently tests whether monetary policy can permanently reduce unemployment. The correct answer is no — in the long run, the Phillips curve is vertical. Also tested: the short-run sacrifice ratio (how much output/employment is lost to reduce inflation by 1 percentage point).

### Monetary Transmission Chain
Repo rate → short-term money market rates → bank lending rates → investment and consumption demand → aggregate demand → output and inflation. The transmission operates through: (a) interest rate channel, (b) credit channel (bank lending channel), (c) asset price channel (wealth effects via equity/housing), (d) exchange rate channel (rupee appreciation reduces exports and imported inflation). Lags in monetary policy: recognition lag, decision lag, implementation lag, and outside lag (effect on economy). The RBI's February 2026 MPC meeting held the repo rate at 5.25% after a cumulative 125 bps reduction since February 2025.

MCQ trap: Reverse repo rate was replaced by SDF (Standing Deposit Facility) as the liquidity corridor floor. SDF differs from reverse repo in that it does not require collateral.

## 2. Growth and Development: Harrod-Domar, Solow, AK Model, Sen, Lewis

### Harrod-Domar Model
Growth rate = s/v, where s is the savings rate and v is the capital-output ratio (ICOR). Harrod's warranted rate of growth (Gw = s/v) is the rate at which entrepreneurs are satisfied with their investment decisions. The natural rate (Gn) is determined by population growth and technical progress. The knife-edge instability problem: if actual growth deviates from Gw, the deviation is self-reinforcing (if G > Gw, excess demand leads to more investment, further widening the gap). Domar assigned a dual role to investment: it generates income (multiplier effect on demand side) and adds to productive capacity (supply side). For balanced growth: G = Gw = Gn.

MCQ trap: The Harrod-Domar model assumes a fixed capital-output ratio (no factor substitution), which is the key reason Solow replaced it.

### Solow Neoclassical Growth Model
Production function: Y = F(K, L) with constant returns to scale. Capital accumulation: ΔK = sY − δK. Steady state: sY/K = δ (investment per worker = depreciation per worker). In steady state, per capita income is constant; total income grows at the rate of exogenous technological progress (g) plus population growth (n). Convergence: conditional convergence — countries with similar structural parameters (s, δ, n) converge to the same steady-state income level. Absolute convergence (unconditional) holds only if all structural parameters are identical — empirically rejected by divergence between rich and poor countries.

Golden Rule savings rate: the savings rate that maximises steady-state consumption per worker. At golden rule: MPK = δ + n + g. Policy cannot affect long-run growth rate in the Solow model (only technology, exogenous) — this is the model's main limitation.

### Endogenous Growth: AK Model
Y = AK, where A captures all factors of production including human capital and technology, and K is broadly defined capital. No diminishing returns to capital (constant returns assumed). Growth rate: g = sA − δ. Unlike Solow, the savings rate permanently affects the growth rate. No convergence — countries with different savings rates grow at permanently different rates. This is a key point the exam tests: endogenous growth predicts divergence, Solow predicts conditional convergence.

MCQ trap: The AK model's key departure from Solow is the assumption of no diminishing returns to capital, not merely the inclusion of human capital.

### Sen's Capability Approach
Amartya Sen's framework shifts the development objective from utility (utilitarian) or income (economic) to capabilities — what people are actually able to do and be. Key concepts: functionings (actual achievements, e.g., being educated, being healthy), capabilities (real opportunities to achieve functionings), agency (ability to act on one's values), and adaptive preferences (the problem that deprived people may adapt their desires downward, making utility-based measures misleading). HDI operationalises a limited version of Sen's framework using longevity, education, and income dimensions. Criticism of HDI: does not capture distribution of human development; the IHDI (Inequality-adjusted HDI) partially corrects this.

### Lewis Dual Sector Model
Arthur Lewis posited a two-sector economy: a traditional (agricultural) sector with surplus labour at subsistence wages, and a modern (capitalist) sector that draws this surplus labour. As long as surplus labour exists in agriculture, wages in manufacturing can be held constant while profits and capital accumulation grow. The turning point (Lewis turning point) occurs when the surplus labour is exhausted, wages begin to rise in both sectors, and the dual economy merges.

MCQ trap: The Lewis model assumes that wages in the capitalist sector are fixed at a premium above the subsistence wage — not at the subsistence wage itself.

## 3. Microeconomics: Consumer Theory, Market Structures, Welfare

### Consumer Theory: Slutsky, Income and Substitution Effects
The Slutsky equation decomposes the total price effect into: (1) substitution effect (movement along the same indifference curve, always negative for own-price) and (2) income effect (shift to a new indifference curve). For normal goods: both effects work in the same direction (price rise → demand falls). For inferior goods: income effect partially offsets substitution effect. For Giffen goods: the income effect dominates the substitution effect in magnitude and is positive, producing an upward-sloping demand curve.

Compensating variation (CV): income change needed to restore original utility after price change. Equivalent variation (EV): income change at new prices equivalent in welfare terms to the price change. The weak axiom of revealed preference (WARP): if bundle A is chosen when B is affordable, then B cannot be chosen when A is affordable at the same price. This proves the substitution effect is always negative without assuming cardinal utility.

MCQ trap: Slutsky substitution effect is evaluated at original utility level (Hicksian: at original quantity bundle). This is different from Hicksian substitution effect (evaluated at original utility level on the indifference curve). The distinction between Slutsky and Hicks compensation generates slightly different demand functions.

### Market Structures: Monopoly DWL and Nash Equilibrium
Monopoly equilibrium: MR = MC determines output; price is read off the demand curve (P > MC). Deadweight loss (DWL) is the welfare triangle between the competitive and monopoly output levels, representing lost consumer and producer surplus. First-degree price discrimination eliminates DWL entirely (monopolist extracts all consumer surplus). Second-degree: quantity discounts. Third-degree: market segmentation by elasticity (MR₁ = MR₂ = MC, so the high-elasticity market gets a lower price).

Cournot duopoly: firms simultaneously choose quantities. Each firm's best response (reaction function) is downward sloping. Nash equilibrium: neither firm can profitably deviate given the other's output. Cournot outcome lies between competitive (maximum output) and monopoly (minimum output). Stackelberg: leader moves first, anticipates follower's reaction function, gains first-mover advantage. Bertrand duopoly with homogeneous goods collapses to competitive outcome (P = MC) — the Bertrand paradox.

Nash equilibrium concept: a strategy profile where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally deviating. Prisoner's dilemma: dominant strategy Nash equilibrium is mutually defective, even though mutual cooperation is Pareto superior — illustrates why cartels are unstable.

### Welfare Analysis
Pareto efficiency: no reallocation can make anyone better off without making someone worse off. First Welfare Theorem: perfectly competitive equilibrium is Pareto efficient. Second Welfare Theorem: any Pareto efficient allocation can be achieved by competitive markets with appropriate lump-sum redistribution. Market failures violating Pareto efficiency: externalities, public goods (non-rival, non-excludable), information asymmetry, and market power.

MCQ trap: The First Welfare Theorem requires perfect competition, complete markets, and no externalities. It does not imply the outcome is equitable — only efficient.

## 4. International Economics: Comparative Advantage, HO Theorem, BOP, J-curve, Marshall-Lerner

### Comparative Advantage and Heckscher-Ohlin
Ricardo: even if a country is absolutely less productive in all goods, trade is beneficial if it specialises in the good where it has a comparative (relative) advantage. Haberler replaced the labour theory of value with opportunity costs, introducing the production possibility frontier. Heckscher-Ohlin (HO) theorem: countries export goods that intensively use their abundant factor. Factor Price Equalisation theorem (Samuelson): free trade equates factor prices across countries through goods trade alone (without factor mobility). Stolper-Samuelson theorem: a rise in the relative price of a good increases the real reward of the factor used intensively in that good and decreases the real reward of the other factor.

Leontief Paradox: US (capital-abundant) exports appeared labour-intensive — challenged HO. Explanations include human capital, natural resources, factor intensity reversals, and demand differences. Factor intensity reversal undermines both the HO theorem and the FPE theorem.

MCQ trap: The HO theorem requires identical tastes and technology across countries. The Stolper-Samuelson theorem is about relative factor rewards, not absolute.

### BOP Identity and Exchange Rate Mechanisms
BOP = Current Account + Capital and Financial Account + Statistical Discrepancy = 0 (by accounting identity). Current Account = Trade Balance (goods) + Services Balance + Primary Income (remittances, investment income) + Secondary Income (transfers). India's current account deficit was 0.8% of GDP in H1 FY26. Forex reserves cover over 11 months of imports as of January 2026.

Marshall-Lerner condition: a devaluation improves the trade balance if the sum of the absolute price elasticities of export demand and import demand exceeds 1 (|εX| + |εM| > 1). If the condition holds, devaluation improves the current account in the long run.

J-curve effect: in the short run after devaluation, the trade balance initially worsens before improving. Contracts are pre-committed in the short run (quantities are sticky, prices adjust immediately), so the value of imports rises while export volumes have not yet risen. Over time, as quantities adjust, the Marshall-Lerner condition reasserts itself and the balance improves.

MCQ trap: The J-curve does not mean devaluation is ineffective — it means the effect is delayed. The Marshall-Lerner condition describes the long-run outcome.

### Terms of Trade
Net barter terms of trade = Px/Pm (ratio of export to import prices). Improvement means exports fetch more relative to imports. Income terms of trade = (Px/Pm) × X (adjusted for export volume). Deteriorating terms of trade is a persistent concern for commodity-dependent developing countries (Prebisch-Singer hypothesis).

## 5. Public Finance: Wagner's Law, Laffer Curve, Fiscal Federalism, 15th FC

### Wagner's Law and Public Expenditure
Adolf Wagner's Law of Increasing State Activity: as per capita income rises, the share of public expenditure in national income rises due to (a) administrative and protective functions expanding with industrial complexity, (b) cultural and welfare expenditures rising as income rises (luxury good argument), and (c) natural monopoly tendencies in infrastructure requiring public provision. Empirical evidence for India is mixed — the law holds broadly but not during periods of fiscal consolidation.

Peacock-Wiseman displacement effect: wars and crises ratchet up public expenditure to new plateaus, as citizens become accustomed to higher tax tolls. Expenditure does not return to pre-crisis levels (hysteresis in public spending).

### Laffer Curve
A conceptual curve showing that tax revenue is zero at both zero tax rate and 100% tax rate, with a maximum at some intermediate rate. The policy implication: reducing tax rates from above the peak increases revenue (supply-side economics). In practice, the peak revenue-maximising rate is empirically controversial and generally estimated to be above current statutory rates in most countries.

MCQ trap: The Laffer curve argument for tax cuts increasing revenue requires the economy to be on the downward-sloping segment — which is an empirical question, not a theoretical certainty.

### Fiscal Federalism and 15th Finance Commission
Musgrave's three functions of fiscal policy: allocation (public goods provision), distribution (income redistribution), and stabilisation (macroeconomic management). Vertical fiscal imbalance: central government collects most taxes; states have large expenditure responsibilities. The Finance Commission addresses this. The 15th Finance Commission (2021-26) recommended 41% devolution of divisible pool taxes to states (down from 42% in the 14th FC, due to the creation of Jammu and Kashmir and Ladakh as Union Territories). Horizontal distribution among states uses criteria: income distance, population (2011 census), area, forest and ecology, demographic performance, and tax effort.

Budget 2026-27 targets fiscal deficit at 4.3% of GDP (down from 4.4% revised estimate for FY26 and 4.8% actual in FY25). Outstanding central government liabilities are estimated at 55.6% of GDP in 2026-27, with a target to reduce to 50% by March 2031.

MCQ trap: The 15th FC used 2011 census population data, not 1971. The earlier FCs used 1971 data to avoid penalising states that successfully reduced fertility.

## 6. Quantitative Methods: OLS BLUE, Index Numbers, Type I/II Errors

### OLS and Gauss-Markov BLUE Conditions
Ordinary Least Squares minimises the sum of squared residuals. Under the Gauss-Markov assumptions, OLS is BLUE (Best Linear Unbiased Estimator): (1) linearity in parameters; (2) random sampling; (3) no perfect multicollinearity; (4) zero conditional mean of errors (E[u|X] = 0); (5) homoscedasticity (constant variance of errors). The fifth assumption is required for OLS to be efficient (minimum variance among linear unbiased estimators). If violated (heteroscedasticity), OLS is still unbiased but not efficient — Weighted Least Squares is preferred.

Multicollinearity: high correlation among regressors. Symptoms: high R² but low t-statistics, coefficient estimates sensitive to small data changes. Does not bias estimates but inflates standard errors (reduces precision). Remedies: drop correlated variable, ridge regression, increase sample size.

Autocorrelation (serial correlation): correlated error terms, typically in time series. Detected by Durbin-Watson test (DW ≈ 2 indicates no autocorrelation; DW < dL indicates positive autocorrelation; DW > 4-dL indicates negative autocorrelation). Remedy: GLS, Cochrane-Orcutt procedure.

MCQ trap: Gauss-Markov does not require normally distributed errors — only for hypothesis testing (t and F tests). OLS under heteroscedasticity is unbiased but inefficient — a commonly confused point.

### Index Numbers: Laspeyres and Paasche Bias
Laspeyres Price Index uses base-period quantities as weights: ΣP₁Q₀/ΣP₀Q₀. It tends to overstate inflation because it ignores substitution away from goods whose prices have risen. Paasche Price Index uses current-period quantities: ΣP₁Q₁/ΣP₀Q₁. It tends to understate inflation because it uses post-substitution quantities. Fisher's Ideal Index is the geometric mean of Laspeyres and Paasche — satisfies both the time reversal and factor reversal tests. India's CPI uses a fixed base-year basket, introducing Laspeyres-type upward bias. The WPI has structural weaknesses: double counting (same goods counted at different production stages), excludes services, excludes imports. PPI (Producer Price Index) is proposed as replacement.

MCQ trap: Laspeyres overstates inflation (upward bias); Paasche understates it (downward bias). The Fisher index is preferred theoretically. CPI India uses Laspeyres-type methodology.

### Type I and Type II Errors in Hypothesis Testing
Type I error (α): rejecting a true null hypothesis (false positive). The probability of a Type I error is the significance level (α = 0.05 means a 5% chance of rejecting a true null). Type II error (β): failing to reject a false null hypothesis (false negative). Power of a test = 1 − β. There is a trade-off: reducing α (making it harder to reject H₀) increases β. The p-value is the probability of observing a test statistic as extreme as the one computed, assuming H₀ is true. If p < α, reject H₀.

## 7. Environmental Economics: Pigouvian Tax, Coase Theorem, Green GDP

### Pigouvian Tax
A tax equal to the marginal external damage at the socially optimal output level. It internalises the negative externality and restores Pareto efficiency. The optimal Pigouvian tax = MEC (marginal external cost) at Q*. Advantage: achieves the socially optimal output in theory. Practical problem: regulators must know the exact marginal damage function, which is difficult to estimate. The alternative market-based instrument is a cap-and-trade system (tradable permits), which achieves the same quantity reduction through market allocation rather than a price signal.

Carbon tax vs cap-and-trade: carbon tax provides price certainty (the government sets the price); cap-and-trade provides quantity certainty (the government sets the total emissions cap). Under uncertainty about the marginal abatement cost curve, the preferred instrument depends on whether the cost or benefit curve is steeper (Weitzman's prices vs quantities result).

### Coase Theorem
Ronald Coase argued that in the absence of transaction costs, private bargaining between the polluter and the polluted party will achieve the socially efficient outcome regardless of the initial assignment of property rights. The theorem implies government intervention is unnecessary if property rights are well-defined and transaction costs are zero. In practice, transaction costs are positive, property rights are often absent or ill-defined, and bargaining breaks down with multiple parties — all of which limit Coase's applicability to small-number cases.

MCQ trap: The Coase theorem does not say the outcome is independent of wealth effects — the distribution of property rights affects income distribution. The allocation of resources is the same, but income is not.

### Green GDP Concept
GDP minus the cost of natural resource depletion and environmental degradation. Conceptual basis in the System of Environmental and Economic Accounts (SEEA), adopted by the UN. Adjustments include: depletion of mineral, energy, and forestry resources; soil degradation; water quality degradation; air pollution costs. India does not yet officially publish a Green GDP. The concept is aligned with genuine savings (World Bank) and the Genuine Progress Indicator (GPI).

## 8. MCQ Trap Summary Across All Areas

The DEPR exam has a predictable pattern of trap questions. The most frequently tested conceptual traps:

Macroeconomics traps: (a) Liquidity trap — in this case, monetary policy is completely ineffective and only fiscal policy works; students confuse this with classical crowding out. (b) Long-run Phillips curve is vertical — permanent reduction in unemployment is impossible through monetary expansion. (c) MPC determines the size of the fiscal multiplier (1/(1-MPC)); a higher MPC means a larger multiplier.

Growth traps: (a) Harrod-Domar model assumes no factor substitution — this is the core limitation, not the knife-edge instability. (b) AK model predicts no convergence; Solow predicts conditional convergence. (c) In the Solow model, a higher savings rate permanently increases the level of per capita income but not the long-run growth rate — a critical distinction.

International economics traps: (a) Comparative advantage holds even if one country is absolutely more productive in everything — this is the point of Ricardo's model. (b) Marshall-Lerner condition applies in the long run; J-curve is the short-run phenomenon. (c) Factor Price Equalisation requires many restrictive assumptions — factor intensity reversals break it.

Public finance traps: (a) Wagner's law predicts the share of public expenditure in income rises with development — it does not predict that absolute public spending rises faster than income (that is a different, stronger claim). (b) Ricardian equivalence says debt-financed and tax-financed spending have the same effect on aggregate demand — this requires very strong assumptions (rational agents, perfect capital markets, finite horizons).

Econometrics traps: (a) Multicollinearity does not cause bias — it inflates variance. (b) OLS under heteroscedasticity is unbiased but not efficient — this is the standard exam distinction. (c) High R² with low t-statistics is a symptom of multicollinearity, not of a poor model.
