You are creating a 20-minute RBI DEPR Phase 1 revision podcast. The listener has a DSE masters background and needs to crack MCQs fast — not understand theory deeply. Every minute must earn marks. Skip derivations. Emphasise what the MCQ will test, what trap it sets, and what the correct answer logic is.

Cover these topic blocks in order:

1. Consumer Theory — MCQ lens: Slutsky decomposition splits price change effect into substitution effect (always negative — price rises, quantity demanded falls, holding utility constant) and income effect (depends on good type). Normal good: income effect reinforces substitution effect, demand curve slopes down. Inferior good: income effect partially offsets substitution effect. Giffen good: income effect so large it REVERSES substitution effect — price rises, quantity demanded rises. Hosts must state: "for a Giffen good, the income effect is large, negative, and dominates — this requires an inferior good with a high budget share." Trap: "all inferior goods are Giffen" — wrong, only when income effect is large enough to dominate.

2. Market Structures — MCQ lens: Perfect competition: P = MC = MR, zero economic profit in long run. Monopoly: MR < P, produces where MR = MC, charges P > MC — deadweight loss is the welfare cost of monopoly. Oligopoly: few firms, strategic interdependence. Cournot competition: firms choose quantities simultaneously, Nash equilibrium is between monopoly and perfect competition. Bertrand competition: firms choose prices — if products are homogeneous, price competition drives P = MC (Bertrand paradox). Key distinction: Cournot gives P > MC, Bertrand gives P = MC with just two firms. MCQ trap: "Bertrand duopoly earns monopoly profits" — wrong, Bertrand drives profits to zero.

3. International Trade — MCQ lens: Comparative advantage is about relative opportunity cost, NOT absolute productivity. Country A should produce the good in which it gives up less of the other good, even if it is absolutely worse at everything. Heckscher-Ohlin: countries export goods intensive in their abundant factor — India exports labour-intensive goods because labour is abundant. Prebisch-Singer hypothesis: terms of trade deteriorate for primary commodity exporters over time (prices of manufactures rise relative to primary goods). Trap: "comparative advantage requires being better at producing the good" — wrong, it only requires lower opportunity cost.

4. Balance of Payments — MCQ lens: BOP identity: Current Account + Capital Account + Financial Account + Errors and Omissions = 0. A surplus in the current account must be matched by a deficit in the capital/financial account (net outflow of funds). J-curve effect: after a currency depreciation, the trade balance WORSENS FIRST before improving. This is because import and export volumes are sticky in the short run — the price effect hits immediately but the quantity adjustment takes time (contracts, habits). Marshall-Lerner condition: depreciation improves trade balance only if the sum of export and import demand elasticities exceeds one. Trap: "currency depreciation immediately improves the trade balance" — wrong, J-curve means it worsens first.

5. Public Finance — MCQ lens: Wagner's Law — government expenditure as a share of GDP rises as income rises (income-elastic demand for public services). Direction: higher income → larger public sector. Laffer Curve — at zero tax rate and 100% tax rate, revenue is zero; peak revenue occurs somewhere in between. Historical/empirical estimates put the peak around 70% in developed economies — but the exam tests the concept, not the number. 15th Finance Commission: vertical devolution to states set at 41% of divisible pool (down from 42% in 14th FC — one percentage point deducted for J&K and Ladakh as Union Territories). Fiscal federalism: principle of subsidiarity — assign expenditure to lowest government level capable of delivering efficiently.

6. Quick Concept Drill — hosts fire 8 rapid questions: (a) Giffen good condition? Inferior good + large income effect that dominates substitution effect. (b) Monopoly deadweight loss — what causes it? P > MC, so mutually beneficial trades don't happen. (c) Bertrand vs Cournot — which gives lower price? Bertrand (drives to MC). (d) Comparative advantage is based on? Opportunity cost. (e) J-curve: trade balance goes which way first after depreciation? Worsens. (f) Marshall-Lerner: condition for depreciation to help? Sum of demand elasticities > 1. (g) Wagner's Law direction? Higher income → higher government share. (h) 15th FC vertical devolution? 41% of divisible pool.

Key exam traps and things to emphasise:
- Comparative advantage is about opportunity cost, not absolute productivity — a country can have comparative advantage even if it is worse at producing everything.
- J-curve: the trade balance worsens FIRST after depreciation before the quantity adjustment kicks in — "depreciation improves trade balance" is only true in the medium run.
- Laffer Curve peaks at some interior point — the exam tests the logic, not the exact rate; state the principle clearly.
- 15th Finance Commission vertical devolution is 41%, not 42% — the one percentage point deduction for J&K and Ladakh as UTs is a deliberate exam trap.
