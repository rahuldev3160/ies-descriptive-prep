Cover these topic blocks in order:

1. IS-LM Framework — MCQ lens: What shifts IS versus what shifts LM? IS shifts on autonomous spending changes (fiscal policy, investment sentiment, exports). LM shifts ONLY when money supply or money demand changes — not when income changes (income moves you along LM). What happens to equilibrium Y and r when the government cuts taxes (IS right → both Y and r rise) versus when the RBI cuts repo rate (LM right → Y rises, r falls). Hosts must name the trap: "income cannot shift LM — that is the most common wrong answer in this section."

2. AD-AS Model — MCQ lens: Distinguish supply shocks from demand shocks. Demand shock: AD shifts, P and Y move same direction. Supply shock: AS shifts, P and Y move opposite directions — that is stagflation. A negative supply shock (oil price spike) raises P and lowers Y simultaneously. MCQ trap: "a sudden rise in oil prices shifts AD to the left" is wrong — it shifts AS left, not AD. Short-run versus long-run AS: LRAS is vertical at potential output.

3. Phillips Curve — MCQ lens: Short-run Phillips Curve shows inflation-unemployment trade-off. Long-run Phillips Curve is vertical at NAIRU — no trade-off. Friedman-Phelps expectation-augmented Phillips Curve: attempts to exploit the trade-off only generate inflation, unemployment returns to natural rate. NAIRU is supply-side, not policy-controllable. Trap: "the Phillips Curve proves monetary policy can permanently lower unemployment" — wrong, only in the short run.

4. Monetarist vs Keynesian — MCQ lens: Keynesians say fiscal multiplier works when liquidity trap holds (LM flat). Monetarists say velocity is stable, MV = PY, money supply is the only effective lever. Crowding out is the Keynesian failure point: fiscal expansion raises r and reduces private investment. Monetary rule vs discretion: Friedman's k-percent rule vs activist policy. MCQ trap: "crowding out is complete under vertical LM" — this is the classical/monetarist position.

5. Monetary Transmission Mechanism — MCQ lens: Chain is repo rate → call money rate → MCLR/EBLR → bank lending rate → credit demand → investment → GDP. Where does it break? High NPAs reduce bank willingness to lend regardless of rate. Low animal spirits suppress investment demand even with cheap credit. MCQ trap: "a repo rate cut always raises investment and GDP" — wrong, transmission can break at multiple points. EBLR (external benchmark, introduced October 2019) transmits faster than MCLR.

Key exam traps and things to emphasise:
- LM shifts when money supply changes, NOT when income changes — income moves you along the existing LM curve.
- The Phillips Curve trade-off disappears in the long run — NAIRU is a constraint, not a policy choice.
- Monetary transmission depends on bank risk appetite and credit demand, not just repo rate — the chain can break at any link.
- Stagflation is always a supply-side story — if P and Y move in opposite directions, the shock hit the AS curve, not the AD curve.
