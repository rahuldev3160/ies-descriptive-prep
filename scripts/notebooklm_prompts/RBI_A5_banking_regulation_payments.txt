Cover these topic blocks in order:

1. Basel III Capital Requirements — India's Implementation: Basel III requires a minimum Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1) ratio of 4.5%, Tier 1 capital ratio of 6%, and Total Capital (CRAR) of 8%. India's RBI mandates a higher CRAR of 9% for scheduled commercial banks — one percentage point above the Basel III minimum. CET1 is the highest quality capital: paid-up equity and retained earnings. Tier 1 also includes Additional Tier 1 (AT1) instruments like perpetual bonds. Tier 2 capital includes subordinated debt and general provisions. The Capital Conservation Buffer of 2.5% (additional CET1) makes the effective minimum 10.5% under Basel III globally; India aligns with this for D-SIBs (Domestic Systemically Important Banks). Hosts must state: "India at 9% CRAR versus Basel's 8% — that one percentage point matters in the exam."

2. Asset Quality — Current Numbers: As of FY26, the Gross NPA ratio of scheduled commercial banks is approximately 2.6% — the lowest in over a decade. Net NPA ratio is approximately 0.6%. The Provisioning Coverage Ratio (PCR) is approximately 76% — meaning 76 paise of every rupee of bad loan has been set aside as provision. PCR rising indicates improving bank health. The improvement since the 2018 peak (GNPA was near 11.6%) reflects IBC resolutions, recoveries, and write-offs. Hosts must explain: GNPA is gross bad loans as a percentage of total advances; Net NPA nets out provisions; PCR shows how much of the bad loan pool is covered.

3. NPA Resolution Framework — IBC vs SARFAESI: The Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code 2016 provides a time-bound corporate resolution process: admission within 14 days, resolution must be completed within 180 days (extendable to 330 days with NCLT approval). If no resolution, liquidation. Haircuts on resolved cases have historically ranged 40-60% — creditors recover 40-60 paise on the rupee. SARFAESI Act 2002 (Securitisation and Reconstruction of Financial Assets and Enforcement of Security Interest): allows secured creditors to seize and sell assets WITHOUT court intervention. Faster than Debt Recovery Tribunals (DRTs). DRTs are specialised courts for debt recovery but slower than SARFAESI for secured assets. Hierarchy for speed: SARFAESI fastest for secured assets, then DRT, then civil courts.

4. Priority Sector Lending — PSL Norms: Banks must lend 40% of Adjusted Net Bank Credit (ANBC) to priority sectors. Categories: Agriculture (18% of ANBC — with sub-targets for small/marginal farmers), MSMEs, Export Credit, Education, Housing, Social Infrastructure, Renewable Energy, Weaker Sections (10% of ANBC). Foreign banks with 20+ branches: same 40% target. Foreign banks with fewer than 20 branches: lower targets. Priority Sector Lending Certificates (PSLCs): banks that over-achieve can sell certificates to banks that under-achieve — a market mechanism. Co-lending model: banks and NBFCs co-originate priority sector loans, sharing risk and regulatory benefit.

5. Payment Systems Hierarchy: RTGS (Real Time Gross Settlement): minimum transaction ₹2 lakh, real-time settlement, available 24×7 (extended in December 2020), used for high-value interbank transactions. NEFT (National Electronic Funds Transfer): no minimum, no maximum, available 24×7 in half-hourly batches since December 2019 — OLD exam papers said NEFT operated in batch mode with cut-off times during banking hours; that is outdated. IMPS (Immediate Payment Service): instant, 24×7, maximum ₹5 lakh per transaction for retail. UPI (Unified Payments Interface): built on IMPS infrastructure, daily limit ₹1 lakh for regular transactions, ₹2 lakh for verified/merchant transactions, cross-border enabled in UAE, Singapore, UK, and others.

6. CBDC — e-Rupee Retail vs Wholesale: RBI launched the digital rupee pilot in two tracks. Wholesale CBDC (e₹-W): launched November 2022, for interbank settlement of government securities transactions — reduces counterparty risk in securities settlement. Retail CBDC (e₹-R): launched December 2022, for general public transactions, works like digital cash (bearer instrument), no interest paid, privacy closer to physical cash than bank deposits. Key distinction: wholesale is for financial institutions settling large transactions; retail is for everyday payments. Not a cryptocurrency — issued and backed by RBI, maintains legal tender status.

Key exam traps and things to emphasise:
